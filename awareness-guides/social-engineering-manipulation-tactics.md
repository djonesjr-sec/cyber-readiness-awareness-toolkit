# Social Engineering Manipulation Tactics

## Purpose

This guide explains common manipulation tactics used in phishing, scams, and social engineering attacks.

Attackers often try to influence emotions and decision-making so people act quickly without verifying the request.

---

## Urgency

Urgency tries to pressure someone into acting immediately before they have time to slow down and verify the request.

### Common Examples

- "Immediate action required"
- "Your account will be suspended today"
- "Respond within 30 minutes"
- "Your payment is overdue"
- "Your mailbox is full"

### Why It Works

People under pressure are more likely to click links, approve requests, or share information without carefully reviewing the message.

### Safer Response

Slow down and verify the request another way before taking action.

---

## Fear

Fear tries to create panic, worry, or concern so someone reacts emotionally instead of carefully verifying the situation.

### Common Examples

- "Suspicious login detected"
- "Your account has been compromised"
- "Legal action may be taken"
- "Your banking account is locked"
- "A virus has been detected on your device"

### Why It Works

Fear can cause people to rush decisions, click unsafe links, share sensitive information, or approve access without verifying the request first.

### Safer Response

Do not panic or immediately trust the message. Verify the alert directly through the official website, app, or support contact.

---

## Authority

Authority tries to gain trust by pretending to be someone important, trusted, or responsible for security, finances, or operations.

### Common Examples

Messages pretending to be:
- IT support
- a manager or executive
- a bank
- Microsoft or Google
- law enforcement
- a delivery company
- a government agency

Example messages:
- "This is the IT department. Confirm your password immediately."
- "Your manager needs this completed urgently."
- "Your account requires immediate verification."

### Why It Works

People are more likely to comply with requests when they believe the message comes from a trusted or important source.

### Safer Response

Verify the request using a trusted phone number, website, or direct conversation before responding or sharing information

---

## Scarcity

Scarcity tries to create pressure by making something appear limited, exclusive, or about to disappear.

### Common Examples

- "Limited time offer"
- "Only a few spots remaining"
- "Final warning"
- "Your access expires today"
- "Act now before this opportunity is gone"
- "Your package will be returned if you do not respond"

### Why It Works

When people believe they may lose access, miss an opportunity, or face a deadline, they are more likely to act quickly without carefully verifying the request.

### Safer Response

Slow down before responding. Verify the message using a trusted source before clicking links, sharing information, or making payments.

---

## Curiosity

Curiosity is used to tempt someone into opening a link, attachment, picture, or video to see something unexpected or interesting.

### Common Examples

- "Is this you in this photo?"
- "You will not believe what happened"
- "Confidential employee report"
- "See who viewed your profile"
- "Important document attached"

### Why It Works

People naturally want answers, hidden information, or unexpected details. Attackers use curiosity to encourage clicks before someone verifies the message.

### Safer Response

Avoid opening unexpected links or attachments. Verify the sender and confirm the message through another trusted method if necessary.

---

## Excitement

Excitement uses rewards, good news, or positive emotions to lower suspicion and encourage quick action.

### Common Examples

- "You won a prize"
- "Claim your free gift card"
- "Congratulations! You were selected"
- "Exclusive reward available now"
- "You have received a refund"

### Why It Works

Positive emotions can reduce skepticism and make people more likely to click links, download files, or share information without verifying the message.

### Safer Response

Be cautious of unexpected prizes, rewards, or financial offers. Verify the message directly through the official company website or app before responding.

---

## Secrecy

Secrecy tries to prevent someone from verifying a request with another person.

### Common Examples

- "Do not tell anyone"
- "This must remain confidential"
- "Handle this privately"
- "Keep this between us"
- "Do not contact IT or management"

### Why It Works

If a person verifies the request with someone else, the scam may be discovered. Attackers use secrecy to isolate the target and reduce the chance of being exposed.

### Safer Response

Be cautious of requests that discourage verification or pressure you to keep something secret. When possible, confirm the request with a trusted person, manager, or official support channel.

---

## Trust or Familiarity

Trust or familiarity is used when attackers pretend to be someone you know or use personal details to make a message seem legitimate.

### Common Examples

- pretending to be a coworker
- pretending to be a family member
- using your real name
- referencing your company, school, or recent activity
- copying the tone or style of someone you know

Example messages:

- "Hey, can you help me with something real quick?"
- "I am in a meeting and cannot talk. Can you buy gift cards?"
- "This document is for you to review."

### Why It Works

People are more likely to trust messages that appear personal, familiar, or connected to their real life.

### Safer Response

Verify unusual requests through another trusted method, especially when money, passwords, MFA codes, sensitive information, or account access is involved.
