# Phishing and Social Engineering Red Flags

## Purpose

Phishing and social engineering attacks try to manipulate people into clicking unsafe links, sharing sensitive information, sending money, approving access, or downloading malware.

---

These attacks are among the most common cybersecurity threats affecting:
- individuals
- families
- small businesses
- large organizations

Instead of attacking technology directly, attackers often target people by creating pressure, fear, urgency, trust, or curiosity.

These attacks may appear through:
- email
- text messages
- phone calls
- QR codes
- social media
- fake websites
- messaging apps

---

## Common Red Flags

Be cautious of messages that include:

- urgent requests for immediate action
- password reset requests you did not expect
- requests for MFA or login codes
- suspicious links or QR codes
- unexpected attachments
- payment requests that cannot be verified
- messages pretending to be IT support, a bank, a coworker, or a delivery company
- pressure to keep something secret
- poor spelling, unusual wording, or strange formatting
- links that do not match the real website

---

## Common Examples

Examples may include:

- "Your account will be suspended today"
- "Click here to secure your account"
- "You missed a package delivery"
- "Confirm your password immediately"
- "Purchase gift cards for an urgent request"
- "Scan this QR code to verify access"

---

## Why These Attacks Work

Social engineering attacks often rely on emotional pressure instead of technical skill.

Attackers may try to create:
- urgency
- fear
- authority
- scarcity
- curiosity
- excitement
- trust
- secrecy

The goal is usually to make someone act quickly before slowing down to verify the request.

---

## Safer Response

Before clicking, replying, paying, or sharing information:

- slow down
- verify the request another way
- avoid clicking links immediately
- contact the company or person directly using a trusted phone number or website
- do not share MFA or login codes
- report suspicious messages when possible

---

## Simple Rule

If a message creates pressure, urgency, fear, secrecy, or excitement, stop and verify before taking action.
